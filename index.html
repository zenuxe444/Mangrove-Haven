Why Are Mangroves Important?

  • Coastal Protection – Mangroves act as natural barriers, reducing wave energy by up to 66%, preventing coastal erosion, and protecting communities from typhoons and storm surges.
  • Habitat for Marine Life – Their complex root systems serve as nurseries for fish, crabs, shrimps, and other marine species, sustaining fisheries and supporting local livelihoods.
  • Carbon Storage – Mangroves absorb and store more carbon per hectare than rainforests, helping fight climate change by reducing greenhouse gases.
  • Water Filtration – Mangroves help maintain water quality by trapping sediments and pollutants, keeping coastal waters clean.
  • Livelihood and Ecotourism – Sustainable mangrove forests create opportunities for fishing, honey production, and eco-tourism, benefiting local economies.

Why Do We Need to Plant Mangroves?

  • Rebuilding Lost Habitats – Reforestation efforts help restore lost mangrove forests, ensuring that marine life continues to thrive.
  • Strengthening Coastal Resilience – More mangroves mean better protection against rising sea levels, strong waves, and extreme weather events.
  • Fighting Climate Change – Expanding mangrove forests increases carbon storage, helping to reduce global warming.
  • Enhancing Biodiversity – Planting mangroves supports ecosystems by providing food and shelter for birds, fish, and other wildlife.

Tawsan Mangroves

Danao City, located in northern Cebu, Philippines, is home to the Tawsan Mangrove Forest Ecopark, which encompasses at least 21 mangrove species, including both naturally grown and hybrid varieties. This ecopark, situated in Barangay Guinsay, was inaugurated on August 28, 2023, and serves as a habitat for various marine life, such as shrimps and fishes.

The name "Tawsan" is derived from the phrase "Tawo sa Hunasan," paying homage to the communities residing along the city’s coastal areas for decades. The ecopark is open daily from 6 a.m. to 5 p.m., with an entrance fee of ₱25 and an additional ecological fee of ₱15, though Danao City residents are exempt from the ecological fee.

Future developments for the ecopark include the construction of an almost three-kilometer-long footbridge for visitors who prefer not to kayak, as well as a floating restaurant to offer a unique dining experience amidst the mangroves.

While specific data on the total area covered by mangroves in Danao City is not readily available, the establishment of the Tawsan Mangrove Forest Ecopark highlights the city’s commitment to preserving and showcasing its mangrove ecosystems.
